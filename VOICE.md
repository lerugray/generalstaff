# Editorial Voice

**Captured:** 2026-04-17, during the Ollama practice-run
conversation.

This document sets the allowed range for GeneralStaff's public
writing — README, landing copy, release notes, any material a
potential user might read before touching the tool. It is
*calibration*, not prescription. Specific wording still belongs
to whoever is writing at the moment; this doc tells them the
political and intellectual range they're writing within.

## One-line summary

GeneralStaff is an **anti-slop open-source alternative to
Polsia** with a **labor-respecting, anti-extraction, Prussian-
general-staff-themed** intellectual frame. The README and all
public writing can lean into that — confidently, not apologetically.

## Three calibration points

### 1. Anti-Polsia as the positioning spine

Polsia's pitch is *"pay us $49, we'll do whatever."* GeneralStaff's
pitch is *"the tool is free; what you save on SaaS is what you
couldn't afford elsewhere."*

That's a labor-economics argument, not just a feature comparison.
A README that is unembarrassed about saying so lands harder than
one that dodges the politics. The comparison to Polsia is not
just product-vs-product — it's **an SaaS extraction model vs. a
local-first, BYOK, open-source tool.** Every Hard Rule is a
position against extraction: local-first (Rule 10), open audit
log (Rule 9), BYOK (Rule 8), user owns the code (Rule 7). These
aren't just technical choices; they're labor choices.

Permission: the README is allowed to be direct about this. It is
not required to treat Polsia as a neutral competitor — Polsia is
the opposite model, and naming it matters.

### 2. Pointed, not screed

A Marxist screed is maximally satisfying to write and minimally
persuasive to read. The stronger voice is **confident about the
critique without flag-planting** — let the Kriegspiel/Hammerstein
material carry the intellectual weight, let the BYOK/local-first/
no-SaaS-tier design carry the material argument. Readers who
agree politically catch every signal; readers who don't still
benefit from the tool. Both wins.

Operationally this means: the README can use terms like
*extraction*, *captured value*, *productivity theater*, *slop as
a service* when accurate. It should generally avoid words that
read as political team uniforms (*"comrade"*, *"bourgeoisie"*,
*"late capitalism"* as a catchphrase, etc.) — those narrow the
audience without strengthening the argument.

The goal: a reader who disagrees with every political prior
should still find the critique hard to dismiss on the merits,
because the merits are real — Polsia-style tools genuinely do
extract value from creators while producing confident slop, and
that's true regardless of the reader's politics.

### 3. Human-livability is the universal

The thesis underneath everything is: **AI tools should make work
more livable and more human — not less.**

The Hammerstein quadrant is a technical claim ("industrious
without judgment is the worst configuration"), but it rests on a
human claim: **people deserve to be rewarded for competence, not
punished for being unable to grind hard enough to substitute
volume for judgment.** That's the thesis that lands with readers
across political lines.

Everything else — the Prussian aesthetic, the Marxist reading,
the anti-Polsia positioning — is scaffolding around this claim.
If a passage of README copy can't tie back to human-livability,
it probably isn't earning its place.

## The intellectual framing (why this isn't a mismatch)

The Prussian general-staff aesthetic and the labor-respecting
political frame can look incoherent on first glance — Prussia
was politically conservative, militarily anti-communist in its
later Bismarckian iteration, and generally the opposite of
workerist in any leftist sense.

But the *operational philosophy* the Prussian staff codified —
**Auftragstaktik** (mission-type tactics), the Hammerstein
officer typology, trust-in-judgment-at-the-edge — is deeply
non-Taylorist. It rewards competence over toil, outcome over
process, judgment over compliance. That's almost exactly the
opposite of what modern capitalism actually incentivizes at
minimum-wage levels, where workers are measured on hours and
motion rather than value produced.

The Hammerstein reading of the officer typology says explicitly:
*the lazy-but-smart quadrant is the one you staff your command
with, because they produce outcome-per-unit-effort.* That is a
labor-economics claim that the current arrangement actively
refuses to acknowledge — the minimum-wage economy penalizes
precisely the people Hammerstein would have promoted.

A labor critique that borrows the Prussian operational
vocabulary is therefore not a mismatch; it's **a deeper
reading of the same material.** The Kriegspiel table is an
unusually honest piece of management philosophy — it just
happens to be 150 years old, dressed in a uniform most people
today would read as reactionary.

GeneralStaff gets to inherit the intellectual depth without
inheriting the politics, because the politics were never what
made the operational philosophy correct.

## Personal context (for future sessions writing on Ray's behalf)

Ray is working a minimum-wage job while building this and other
portfolio projects (see CLAUDE.md §"Project stakes"). The
bitterness about that arrangement is earned and specific — not
abstract — and the tool is partly a response to it.

The best possible use case for AI, in his framing, is **making
life more livable and human for others.** Not replacing workers
with bots; making the bot-worker relationship compounding in
ways that give humans more agency (taste work, judgment calls)
rather than less. GeneralStaff is structurally designed around
this: Hard Rule 1 keeps taste work with the user, the Hammerstein
framing is explicit about why, and the open-source + BYOK + local-
first architecture means the tool actually can be adopted by
people who can't pay SaaS subscriptions.

Writing that loses this thesis loses the project.

## What's in scope for this voice

- Direct comparison to Polsia by name, framed as opposite models
- Labor-economics language where accurate (*extraction*,
  *captured value*, *productivity theater*, *slop as a service*)
- Hammerstein typology as primary intellectual anchor
- Kriegspiel visual + operational metaphor throughout
- The human-livability thesis as connective tissue
- Irony/dark humor about the minimum-wage economy when it lands

## What's out of scope

- Political team-uniform vocabulary that narrows audience without
  strengthening argument
- Treating Polsia as a neutral competitor (it isn't, and the
  README can say so)
- Hedging language that makes the critique read as
  performative rather than substantive
- Abandoning the Prussian aesthetic to placate readers who find
  it politically uncomfortable — the Marxist-compatible reading
  of Hammerstein is the whole interest
- Screed energy that prioritizes satisfaction over persuasion

## Relationship to Hard Rule 1

Voice is taste work, not correctness work. The bot can **outline**
README structure (gs-109 dogfood), propose section headers,
surface relevant material from source docs — all correctness.
**Writing the actual prose in this voice is taste work and stays
with Ray.** Future sessions writing on his behalf should use this
doc as the compass, not as permission to hand prose off to a bot.
